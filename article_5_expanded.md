The Banking System’s Fundamental Problem

To understand this, we need to start with a simple question: How do banks create money?

The answer surprises most people. Banks don’t just lend out deposits they receive. Banks actually create money when they make loans.

The Money Creation Process

Here’s a concrete example:

You deposit £1,000 in a bank

The bank has a liability (it owes you £1,000)

You have an asset (a deposit worth £1,000)

Money in the system: £1,000

The bank lends £900 to a business

The business receives a loan (an asset)

The bank has a claim on the business (an asset)

The business now has £900 in deposits (a liability to the bank)

Money in the system: £1,000 (your deposit) + £900 (business deposit) = £1,900

The result: Money has been created

You still have £1,000 in deposits

The business has £900 in deposits

Total money in the system: £1,900 (up from £1,000)

The bank created £900 of new money through lending

This is how the modern economy works. Banks create money through lending. This money creation is essential for economic growth. Without it, the economy would be limited to the amount of physical currency in circulation, which would be far too small to support modern commerce.

Why This Matters

This money creation process is not a side effect of banking. It’s the core function. Banks exist to create credit. Credit creation drives economic growth. When businesses can borrow, they can invest in new equipment, hire workers, and expand operations. This creates jobs and economic growth.

But here’s the critical insight: This system depends on deposits.

Banks can only lend if they have deposits to lend against. Deposits are the fuel that powers credit creation. Without deposits, banks can’t lend. Without lending, the economy stagnates.

This is why the Federal Reserve closely monitors bank deposits. Deposits are the leading indicator of credit creation, which is the leading indicator of economic growth.

The Yield-Bearing Money Problem

Now imagine what happens when yield-bearing money is introduced:

The Deposit Flight

A bank offers 0.5% on deposits

You have £1,000 in the bank

You earn £5 per year

The bank pays you this interest from its profits

A stablecoin issuer offers 5% yield

You move your £1,000 to the stablecoin

You earn £50 per year

The bank loses your deposit

The bank loses the ability to lend against your £1,000

The bank’s lending capacity collapses

The bank has fewer deposits

The bank can lend less

Credit creation declines

Economic growth slows

This is what will happen once the GENIUS Act is modified. Deposits will flee banks to high-yield stablecoins. Banks’ deposit bases will contract dramatically.

The Vicious Cycle

Here’s where it gets worse. The credit collapse triggers a vicious cycle:

Deposits flee banks → Banks reduce lending

Banks reduce lending → Businesses can’t borrow → Economic growth slows

Economic growth slows → Unemployment rises → Incomes decline

Incomes decline → People have less money to deposit in banks

People have less money → Deposits decline further → Banks reduce lending further

The cycle repeats → Economic collapse

This is exactly what happened in the Great Depression. Banks failed. Deposits disappeared. Credit creation collapsed. The economy contracted by 25%. Unemployment reached 25%.

The Historical Parallel

This isn’t new. This is exactly what happened in the 1930s during the Great Depression.

When the banking system fragmented (due to bank failures and loss of confidence), credit creation collapsed. Without credit, the economy contracted. Unemployment soared. The depression deepened.

The Great Depression lasted a decade. It took the Second World War to finally end it. Millions of people suffered. Entire economies were destroyed.

It took decades to recover. And the solution was to enforce monetary singularity: one currency, one central bank, one banking system.

The Federal Reserve was created in 1913 (before the Depression) to prevent exactly this scenario. The FDIC was created in 1933 (during the Depression) to guarantee deposits and prevent bank runs. The Glass-Steagall Act was passed in 1933 to separate commercial banking from investment banking.

All of these reforms were designed to enforce monetary singularity and prevent the fragmentation that leads to banking collapse.
