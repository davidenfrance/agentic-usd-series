## Part 1: The FX Risk Problem (You're Partially Right)

### Your Point

A UK pension fund with GBP liabilities would face a currency mismatch if it holds USD stablecoins. If sterling depreciates against the dollar, the fund's USD holdings become more valuable in GBP terms—but this doesn't help if the fund's liabilities are in GBP. Conversely, if sterling appreciates, the fund's USD holdings lose value in GBP terms, creating a loss. This is a fundamental mismatch.

### Where You're Right

For **liability-matched portfolios**, this is a serious problem. A pension fund that has promised to pay £1 million in pensions in 10 years cannot simply hold USD stablecoins without creating currency risk. Your point is economically sound.

### Where the Argument Gets More Complex

However, the threat doesn't come from liability-matched institutions. It comes from **non-liability-matched actors**:

#### **1. Corporate Treasury Operations**

A multinational corporation (e.g., HSBC, Shell, Unilever) operates globally. Its liabilities are not purely in GBP. It has:
- USD revenues from US operations
- EUR revenues from European operations
- JPY revenues from Japanese operations
- And so on

For such a corporation, holding USD stablecoins is **not** a currency mismatch—it's a **natural hedge**. The corporation's USD revenues naturally match USD holdings. In fact, holding USD stablecoins might be **better** than holding USD in traditional bank accounts because:
- 24/7 settlement (no weekend/holiday delays)
- Programmable payments (automatic settlement of supply chain obligations)
- Yield pass-through (if GENIUS is amended)
- No counterparty risk (backed by US Treasuries, not bank deposits)

**Scale**: The UK's multinational corporations hold approximately **£200-300 billion in USD working capital**. Even a 20% shift to USD stablecoins would represent £40-60 billion in drain from the domestic financial system.

#### **2. Institutional Investors with Floating Liabilities**

Some institutional investors don't have fixed GBP liabilities. Consider:
- **Insurance companies**: Liabilities are in multiple currencies (they insure global operations)
- **Asset managers**: No fixed liabilities; they manage assets for clients
- **Sovereign wealth funds**: No fixed liabilities; they invest for long-term returns
- **Endowments**: Liabilities are loosely defined (spending targets, not fixed obligations)

For these actors, holding USD stablecoins is not a currency mismatch. It's a **portfolio choice**. And if USD stablecoins offer better terms (programmability, settlement speed, yield), they might be preferred to gilts.

#### **3. The Retail Saver Problem (Your Implicit Point)**

You're right that retail savers with GBP liabilities (mortgages, living expenses) shouldn't hold USD stablecoins. But here's the problem: **retail savers might not understand this**.

Consider a UK retail investor who:
- Reads that Agentic USD offers 5% yield (if GENIUS is amended)
- Reads that it's backed by US Treasuries (safe)
- Reads that it's programmable and can be used for payments
- Doesn't fully understand currency risk

This investor might allocate a portion of their savings to Agentic USD, not realizing the FX risk. If enough retail savers do this, it creates a drain on sterling-denominated assets (gilts, bank deposits).

**The key insight**: The threat isn't from rational, liability-matched actors. It's from **irrational actors, non-liability-matched actors, and actors with multi-currency operations**.

## Part 2: The Yield Problem (You've Identified a Real Gap)

### Your Point

GENIUS does not currently permit yield on stablecoins. Therefore, why would anyone give up the yield on gilts (currently 4-5%) for zero-yield stablecoins?

### Where You're Right

Under the current GENIUS framework, your point is **completely valid**. A rational actor comparing:
- **Gilts**: 4-5% yield, GBP denomination, government backing
- **Agentic USD**: 0% yield (no pass-through), USD denomination, US Treasury backing

...would clearly choose gilts. The yield differential is too large to ignore.

### Where the Argument Needs Revision

However, I believe we've made an error in our analysis. The threat from Agentic USD is **not** that it will immediately replace gilts as a yield-bearing asset. The threat is that **GENIUS will be amended to permit yield pass-through**.

#### **Why GENIUS Will Likely Be Amended**

The current GENIUS framework prohibits yield pass-through to prevent stablecoins from being classified as securities. However:

1. **Regulatory Pressure**: If Agentic USD stablecoins become widely used but offer zero yield, they will be at a competitive disadvantage to other digital assets (CBDCs, other stablecoins). The US government will face pressure to amend GENIUS to permit yield pass-through.

2. **International Competition**: The European Central Bank is developing the digital euro, which will likely offer yield. The Bank of England is developing the digital pound, which will likely offer yield. If Agentic USD doesn't offer yield, it will lose market share to these alternatives.

3. **Market Demand**: If Agentic USD becomes widely used for corporate treasury operations and institutional investment, there will be demand for yield. Market pressure will drive regulatory change.

4. **Technical Feasibility**: There's no technical reason why Agentic USD can't offer yield pass-through. The regulatory prohibition is a policy choice, not a technical constraint.

#### **The Amended GENIUS Scenario**

Imagine that in 2027-2028, GENIUS is amended to permit yield pass-through. Agentic USD can now offer 4.5-5% yield (pass-through from US Treasuries). Now the comparison becomes:

- **Gilts**: 4-5% yield, GBP denomination, government backing, illiquid (settlement takes 2-3 days)
- **Agentic USD**: 4.5-5% yield, USD denomination, US Treasury backing, liquid (settlement takes seconds)

For a multinational corporation or institutional investor with multi-currency operations, **Agentic USD becomes superior** because:
- Comparable yield
- Better liquidity
- Programmability
- 24/7 settlement
- No counterparty risk

**This is where the gilt drain happens.**

#### **Your Implicit Critique is Valid**

You're right to point out that under the **current** GENIUS framework, our argument doesn't hold water. The threat is contingent on GENIUS being amended. We should have made this explicit.

## Part 3: The Threat to Major Economies (You've Identified the Real Debate)

### Your Point

Smaller economies with unstable currencies face inflation risk, so they have a natural incentive to hold USD. But major economies like the UK have stable currencies and deep gilt markets. Why would they face the same threat?

### Where You're Right

For **retail savers and liability-matched institutions**, you're correct. The threat is primarily to emerging markets.

### Where the Threat to Major Economies Emerges

However, there are several mechanisms through which even major economies face a threat:

#### **Mechanism 1: The Corporate Treasury Drain**

As discussed above, multinational corporations operating in major economies might shift USD working capital to Agentic USD. This doesn't directly drain the gilt market, but it drains the domestic banking system of USD deposits. This has indirect effects:

- **Reduced credit creation**: Banks that lose USD deposits have less capital to lend
- **Higher borrowing costs**: Reduced credit availability drives up interest rates
- **Reduced gilt demand**: If banks can't lend, they hold fewer gilts as reserves

**Scale**: If UK multinationals shift £40-60 billion in USD working capital to Agentic USD, the UK banking system loses that capital. The impact on credit creation and gilt demand could be significant.

#### **Mechanism 2: The Velocity Problem**

Here's where our argument gets stronger: **Even if the volume of Agentic USD is small, its velocity could be very high.**

A £1 billion of Agentic USD held by AI agents might circulate 100 times per year (constantly being deployed and redeployed). By contrast, £1 billion of gilts held by pension funds might circulate only 0.1 times per year (held to maturity).

This means that even a small volume of Agentic USD could have outsized economic effects through its high velocity. The economic activity it enables could be 1000x greater than the volume itself.

**Why this matters for major economies**: High-velocity Agentic USD could enable new forms of financial activity (AI-driven trading, algorithmic market-making, etc.) that create localized inflation in digital assets and financial services. This could affect monetary policy transmission and create financial stability risks.

#### **Mechanism 3: The Regulatory Arbitrage Problem**

If Agentic USD becomes widely used globally, and if it operates on blockchain networks that are difficult to regulate, then regulatory authorities in major economies lose visibility into financial activity.

As we discussed in Article 1, the "blinding effect" of decentralized finance creates a visibility crisis for central banks. Even if the volume of Agentic USD is small, the loss of visibility could make monetary policy less effective.

**Why this matters for major economies**: A central bank that can't see 10% of financial activity in its jurisdiction loses the ability to effectively manage monetary policy. This is a threat even if the 10% is not the primary driver of economic activity.

#### **Mechanism 4: The Systemic Risk Problem**

Here's the strongest argument for why major economies face a threat:

If Agentic USD becomes the backstop for global insurance markets (as we argued in Article 8), and if global insurance markets become dependent on Agentic USD, then a crisis in Agentic USD could trigger a global insurance crisis.

**Why this matters for major economies**: Even if UK institutions don't directly hold Agentic USD, they're exposed to it indirectly through:
- Insurance policies backed by Agentic USD
- Counterparty exposure to institutions that hold Agentic USD
- Derivative exposure to Agentic USD price movements
- Exposure to AI-driven trading strategies that use Agentic USD

A crisis in Agentic USD could trigger a systemic crisis in major economies through these indirect channels.

## Part 4: Addressing the Core Tension

### The Honest Assessment

You've identified a genuine tension in our argument. Here's the honest version:

**Our argument is strongest for emerging markets and weakest for major economies.**

For emerging markets:
- ✅ Clear incentive to hold USD (inflation protection)
- ✅ Clear threat to local currency (dollarization)
- ✅ Clear mechanism (retail savers fleeing local currency)

For major economies:
- ❓ Weaker incentive to hold USD (stable currency)
- ❓ Unclear mechanism (why would liability-matched institutions hold USD?)
- ❓ Contingent on GENIUS amendment (to permit yield pass-through)

### Where Our Argument Needs Strengthening

We should have been more explicit about:

1. **The GENIUS amendment assumption**: Our threat assessment assumes GENIUS will be amended to permit yield pass-through. This is plausible but not certain.

2. **The corporate treasury channel**: The real threat to major economies comes from multinational corporations shifting working capital, not from retail savers or pension funds.

3. **The velocity/visibility problem**: The threat is not primarily about volume drain, but about velocity and visibility loss.

4. **The systemic risk channel**: The threat to major economies is primarily indirect (through insurance markets and counterparty exposure), not direct.
