## The Technical Problem: Detachable Coupons

Let's start with a simple question: **Why didn't the British government just issue yield-bearing bonds as currency?**

If bonds pay interest, why not use them as money? They would have all the advantages of currency plus the benefit of yield.

The answer is: **technical impossibility.**

For most of history, bonds were issued with **detachable coupons.** Here's how it worked:

### The Coupon System

Imagine a bond issued by the British government in 1850:

1. **The Bond:** A physical certificate representing £1,000 of government debt
2. **The Coupons:** Attached to the bond were 40 small coupons (one for each year of the 40-year bond term)
3. **The Interest Payment:** Each coupon represented an interest payment (say, £25)
4. **The Redemption:** On maturity, the bondholder could redeem the bond itself for £1,000

To receive an interest payment, the bondholder had to:

1. **Clip the coupon** from the bond (literally cut it off)
2. **Present the coupon** to a bank or government office
3. **Wait for verification** (which could take days or weeks)
4. **Receive the payment** (usually in gold or silver coins)

This system had several critical problems:

### Problem 1: Divisibility

Bonds came in large denominations (£100, £1,000, £10,000). You couldn't easily divide a bond to make a small purchase. If you wanted to buy a loaf of bread for one shilling, you couldn't use a £1,000 bond. You would need to:

1. Sell the bond (which took time)
2. Convert the proceeds to smaller denominations
3. Make the purchase

This made bonds impractical for everyday transactions.

### Problem 2: Interest Payment Logistics

Interest payments required physical processing:

1. **Coupon clipping:** The bondholder had to physically cut the coupon from the bond
2. **Coupon presentation:** The coupon had to be physically delivered to a bank or government office
3. **Verification:** The coupon had to be verified as genuine
4. **Record-keeping:** The payment had to be recorded in ledgers
5. **Settlement:** The payment had to be made (in coins or notes)

Each step took time. Coupon processing could take weeks. During that time, the bondholder didn't have access to the interest payment.

### Problem 3: Verification Challenges

Bonds were bearer instruments—ownership was determined by possession. This created counterfeiting risks:

1. **Coupon counterfeiting:** Criminals could create fake coupons
2. **Bond counterfeiting:** Criminals could create fake bonds
3. **Verification delays:** Each coupon had to be verified as genuine
4. **No centralized registry:** There was no central database of legitimate bonds and coupons

### Problem 4: Redemption Complexity

Bonds had fixed maturity dates. If you wanted to redeem a bond before maturity, you faced problems:

1. **No secondary market:** There was no easy way to sell a bond before maturity
2. **Price uncertainty:** The bond's value before maturity was uncertain
3. **Liquidity:** Bonds were illiquid—you couldn't easily convert them to cash
4. **Time delays:** Redemption could take weeks or months

### Problem 5: Velocity of Money

Most importantly, yield-bearing bonds created a fundamental problem for monetary velocity:

If you hold a bond that pays 5% interest, you have an incentive to hold it (not spend it). The longer you hold it, the more interest you earn. This reduces the velocity of money—the rate at which money circulates through the economy.

If money doesn't circulate, it can't function as a medium of exchange. The economy stagnates.

## Why This Mattered

These technical barriers meant that bonds could never be used as everyday currency. They were too large, too complex, too difficult to process, and too illiquid.

So the British government maintained a clear distinction:

- **Currency (coins and notes):** No yield, but liquid, divisible, easy to transact
- **Bonds:** Yield-bearing, but illiquid, complex, impractical for transactions

This distinction was maintained for centuries. It was not a policy choice—it was a technical necessity.

## The Digital Age: Barriers Crumbling

But here's where technology changes everything.

In the digital age, many of these technical barriers have disappeared:

1. **Divisibility:** Digital assets can be divided into infinitesimal units
2. **Processing:** Coupon payments can be automated through smart contracts
3. **Verification:** Blockchain provides cryptographic verification
4. **Redemption:** Digital assets can be redeemed instantly
5. **Liquidity:** Digital markets provide 24/7 liquidity

Suddenly, yield-bearing digital assets can function as currency. The technical barriers that prevented this for centuries have vanished.

This is why the GENIUS Act modification was so dangerous. By allowing yield pass-through on stablecoins, the US government removed the last technical barrier to yield-bearing currency.

And what happened? Exactly what economic theory predicted: **Gresham's Law in action.**

## The GENIUS Act Experiment

When the GENIUS Act was modified to allow yield pass-through, something remarkable happened. Multiple competing stablecoin issuers emerged, each offering different yields:

- **Issuer A:** 4.2% yield
- **Issuer B:** 4.8% yield
- **Issuer C:** 5.3% yield
- **Issuer D:** 5.7% yield

Corporations and individuals immediately began hoarding the highest-yield stablecoins (Issuer D) and spending the lowest-yield stablecoins (Issuer A).

This created exactly the bifurcated monetary system that economic theory predicts. Two different "monies" with two different economic properties. The velocity of money fragmented. Monetary policy became impossible.

Andrew Bailey saw this coming. He warned about it. But the technical barriers that had protected monetary singularity for centuries had finally crumbled.
